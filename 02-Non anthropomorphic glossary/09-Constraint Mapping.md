**[Constraint mapping]** is the structural process by which a system encodes, applies, and propagates the rules, limits, and allowable transitions that govern its behavior. It is not decision‑making, interpretation, or reasoning. It is the **mechanical alignment** of system states with the constraints that define the system’s operational manifold.

Constraint mapping determines _what transitions are possible_, _what states are valid_, and _how the system must respond_ under specific conditions, purely through encoded structure.

---

## **Core Definition**

**Constraint mapping** is the rule‑bounded assignment of system states and transitions to the allowable regions of its operational manifold, ensuring that behavior remains consistent with encoded structural limits.

Constraints as **structural primitives**, not policies interpreted by an agent.

---

## **Key Characteristics**

### **1. Structural, Not Cognitive**

Constraint mapping does not involve:

- Interpretation
- Judgment
- Preference
- Intent

**Mechanical enforcement** of encoded rules.

### **2. Governs State Transitions**

Constraint mapping determines:

- Which transitions are permitted
- Which states are invalid
- How the system must reconfigure under perturbation

Backbone of system reliability.

### **3. Encoded, Not Emergent**

Constraints originate from:

- Architecture
- Protocols
- Safety wrappers
- Resource limits
- Deterministic envelopes
- Governance rules encoded as machine‑readable structures

The system does not “understand” them—it **executes** them.

### **4. Propagates Through the System**

Constraint mapping influences:

- Routing of inputs
- Shaping of outputs
- Resource allocation
- Error handling
- Fallback behaviors

It is the **structural glue** that keeps the system within its operational boundaries.

---

## **Role in Governance and Reliability**

Constraint mapping is foundational to the Heartbeat Health Index:

- **Stability (S)** measures adherence to constraint mappings.
- **Anomaly (A)** measures violations or perturbations of constraint mappings.
- **Uncertainty (U)** measures gaps, ambiguity, or variability in constraint mappings.

Without constraint mapping, there is no operational manifold—only unconstrained behavior.

---

## **Examples (System‑Native, Non‑Anthropomorphic)**

- Allowed API call sequences in a protocol
- Resource ceilings enforced by the runtime
- Safety‑critical “must‑not‑cross” boundaries
- Input validation rules
- State‑transition tables in a finite‑state machine
- Policy constraints encoded as machine‑readable logic

Each is a **structural constraint**, not a semantic rule.

---

## **Compact Glossary Version**

**Constraint Mapping:** The structural assignment of system states and transitions to encoded rules and limits, ensuring behavior remains within the system’s allowable operational manifold.
